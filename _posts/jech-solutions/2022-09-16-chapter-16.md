  <a name="ex16.1"></a>
  <h3 class="semester-title">Exercise 16.1</h3>
  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    By Lemma 16.3 we know that $B(P) * B(\dot{Q})$ is a complete Boolean algebra. Thus, it suffices to show that $P * \dot{Q}$ embeds densely in $B(P) * B(\dot{Q})$, by the uniqueness of Boolean completion.<br/>
    <br/>
    For simplicity we assume $P$ is separative and $\forces_P \dot{Q}$ is separative. For each $(p,\dot{q}) \in P * \dot{Q}$, we define $e : P * \dot{Q} \to B(P) * B(\dot{Q})$ such that $e(p,\dot{q}) = [\dot{c}]$, where it is the unique $\dot{c} \in D$ such that:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert \dot{c} = \dot{q}\Vert  = p, \;\; \Vert \dot{c} = \dot{0}\Vert  = -p
    \end{align*}$$
    This $e$ is well-defined by the completeness of $B(P)$ and fullness of $V^B$, and clearly $e$ is injective.<br/>
    <br/>
    We shall show that $e$ is a dense embedding (i.e. it satisfies the conditions in Lemma 14.11).
    <ol style="margin: 8px 0; padding-left: 1.5em;">
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">Since $1_{P * \dot{Q}} = (1_{P},\dot{1}_{Q})$, clearly $e(1_{P * \dot{Q}}) = [\check{1}_{Q}]$.</li>
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">Suppose $(p_1,\dot{q}_1) \leq (p_2,\dot{q}_2)$, so $p_1 \leq p_2$ and $p_1 \forces \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{q}_2$, i.e. $p_1 \leq \Vert \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{q}_2\Vert $. If $e(p_1,\dot{q}_1) = [\dot{c}_1]$ and $e(p_2,\dot{q}_2) = [\dot{c}_2]$, then:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \Vert \dot{c}_1 \leq \dot{c}_2\Vert  &= \Vert \dot{c}_1 = \dot{q}_1 \wedge \dot{c}_2 = \dot{q}_2 \wedge \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{q}_2\Vert  \\
        &\eqbreak + \Vert \dot{c}_1 = \dot{0} \wedge \dot{c}_2 = \dot{q}_2 \wedge \dot{0} \leq \dot{q}_2\Vert  \\
        &\eqbreak + \Vert \dot{c}_1 = \dot{q}_1 \wedge \dot{c}_2 = \dot{0} \wedge \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{0}\Vert  \\
        &\eqbreak + \Vert \dot{c}_1 = \dot{0} \wedge \dot{c}_2 = \dot{0} \wedge \dot{0} \leq \dot{0}\Vert  \\
        &= p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot \Vert \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{q}_2\Vert  + (-p_1) \cdot p_2 \cdot \Vert \dot{0} \leq \dot{q}_2\Vert  \\
        &\eqbreak + \underbrace{p_1 \cdot (-p_2)}_{=0} \cdot \Vert \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{0}\Vert  + (-p_1) \cdot (-p_2) \cdot \Vert \dot{0} \leq \dot{0}\Vert  \\
        &\geq p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot p_1 + (-p_1) \cdot p_2 \cdot 1 + (-p_1) \cdot (-p_2) \cdot 1 \\
        &= p_1 \cdot p_2 + (-p_1) \cdot p_2 + (-p_2) \\
        &= 1
        \end{align*}$$
        On the other hand, suppose $e(p_1,\dot{q}_1) \leq e(p_2,\dot{q}_2)$, so $\Vert \dot{c}_1 \leq \dot{c}_2\Vert  = 1$. Expanding similarly gives:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot \Vert \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{q}_2\Vert  + (-p_1) \cdot p_2 \cdot 1 + p_1 \cdot (-p_2) \cdot \Vert \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{0}\Vert  + (-p_1) \cdot (-p_2) \cdot 1 = 1
        \end{align*}$$
        Note that $\Vert \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{0}\Vert  = \Vert \dot{q}_1 = \dot{0}\Vert  \geq (-p_1)$, so $p_1 \cdot (-p_2) \cdot \Vert \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{0}\Vert  = 0$. Hence:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot \Vert \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{q}_2\Vert  + (-p_1) = 1
        \end{align*}$$
        This implies that $p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot \Vert \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{q}_2\Vert  \geq p_1$, so $\Vert \dot{q}_1 \leq \dot{q}_2\Vert  \geq p_1$ and $p_2 \geq p_1$.</li>
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">Let $[\dot{c}] \in B(P) * B(\dot{Q})$. We have:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \Vert \dot{c} \in B(\dot{Q})\Vert  = 1 \iff \Vert (\exists \dot{d} \in B(\dot{Q})) \, \dot{c} = \dot{d}\Vert  = 1
        \end{align*}$$
        Since $V^B$ is full, there exists a $\dot{d} \in B(\dot{Q})$ such that $\Vert \dot{c} = \dot{d}\Vert  = 1$. Since $\Vert \dot{Q} \text{ is dense in } B(\dot{Q})\Vert  = 1$, there exists a $\dot{q} \in \dot{Q}$ such that $\Vert \dot{q} \leq \dot{d}\Vert  = 1$. Then clearly $e(1_P,\dot{q}) \leq [\dot{c}]$.</li>
    </ol>
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>

  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    Consider the map $\pi : P \times Q \to P * \check{Q}$ by:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \pi(p,q) = (p,\check{q})
    \end{align*}$$
    Clearly $\pi$ is injective. To see that $\pi$ is dense, let $(p,\dot{q}) \in P * \check{Q}$ be such that $\forces_P \dot{q} \in \check{Q}$. In other words, $\Vert \dot{q} \in \check{Q}\Vert  = 1$. Since $Q \in V$:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    1 = \Vert \dot{q} \in \check{Q}\Vert  = \sum_{q \in Q} \Vert \dot{q} = \check{q}\Vert
    \end{align*}$$
    Thus $\Vert \dot{q} = \check{q}\Vert  = p'$ for some $p' > 0$ and $q \in Q$. Furthermore, there must be at least one such $p'$ such that $p'' := p \cdot p' \neq 0$, for otherwise $\Vert \dot{q} \in \check{Q}\Vert  \leq 1 - p$. Thus $p'' \forces_P \Vert \dot{q} = \check{q}\Vert $, and hence $(p'',\check{q}) \leq (p,\dot{q})$.
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>

  <h3 class="semester-title">Exercise 16.3</h3>
  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    We first need to show that $\Vert \dot{I} \text{ is an ideal}\Vert  = 1$, so that it makes sense to write $D/\dot{I}$.
    <ol style="margin: 8px 0; padding-left: 1.5em;">
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">We first have:
        $$
        \begin{gather*}
        \Vert 0 \in \dot{I}\Vert  = \sum\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot 0 = 0\rbrace = \sum B = 1 \\
        \Vert 1 \in \dot{I}\Vert  = \sum\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot 1 = 0\rbrace = \sum \lbrace 0\rbrace = 0
        \end{gather*}$$
      </li>
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">Fix $u, v \in D$, and suppose $\Vert u \in \dot{I}\Vert  = x$ and $\Vert v \in \dot{I}\Vert  = y$. We see that:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \Vert u + v \in \dot{I}\Vert  &= \bb{-\sum\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot v = 0\rbrace} + \sum\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot (u + v) = 0\rbrace \\
        &= \sum\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot u + b \cdot v = 0\rbrace \\
        &= \sum\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot u = 0 \wedge b \cdot v = 0\rbrace
        \end{align*}$$
        Since $B$ is complete, there exists some $z \in B$ such that $z = \sum\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot u = 0 \wedge b \cdot v = 0\rbrace$. Since $x \cdot y \cdot u = x \cdot y \cdot v = 0$, clearly $z \geq x \cdot y$. On the other hand, since $\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot u = 0 \wedge b \cdot v = 0\rbrace \subseteq \lbrace b \in B : b \cdot u = 0\rbrace$, we have $z \leq x$. By the same reasoning $z \leq y$, so $z = x \cdot y$. This gives:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        &\eqbreak \Vert (u \in \dot{I} \wedge v \in \dot{I}) \to u + v \in \dot{I}\Vert  \\
        &= -\Vert u \in \dot{I}\Vert  + (-\Vert v \in \dot{I}\Vert ) + \Vert u + v \in \dot{I}\Vert  \\
        &= -x + (-y) + x \cdot y \\
        &= 1
        \end{align*}$$
      </li>
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">Fix $u, v \in D$ such that $v \leq u$. Suppose $\Vert u \in \dot{I}\Vert  = x$. Then $\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot u = 0\rbrace \subseteq \lbrace b \in B : b \cdot v = 0\rbrace$, so:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        x := \sum\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot u = 0\rbrace \leq \sum\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot v = 0\rbrace =: y
        \end{align*}$$
        Therefore:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \Vert u \in \dot{I} \to v \in \dot{I}\Vert  = -x + y \geq -x + x = 1
        \end{align*}$$
      </li>
    </ol>
    We now show that in $V^B$, $D : B \cong D/\dot{I}$. If $\dot{F}$ is a filter, then $D/\dot{F}$ consists of equivalence classes $[d]$ under:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    u \sim v \iff u \leftrightarrow v \in F
    \end{align*}$$
    where $u \leftrightarrow v = (u \cdot v) + ((-u) \cdot (-v)) = (u + (-v)) \cdot (v + (-u))$. Thus, to show that $D : B \cong D/\dot{I}$, it suffices to show that:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert u \symdiff v \in \dot{I}\Vert  = \Vert u \leftrightarrow v \in \dot{F}\Vert
    \end{align*}$$
    For any $w \in D$:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert w \in \dot{F}\Vert  &= \Vert (\exists b \in B)(b \in \dot{G} \wedge b \leq w)\Vert  \\
    &= \sum_{b \leq w} \Vert b \in \dot{G}\Vert  \\
    &= \sum_{b \leq w} b \\
    &= \sum\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot (-w) = 0\rbrace
    \end{align*}$$
    Observing that $u \symdiff v = -(u \leftrightarrow v)$:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert u \leftrightarrow v \in \dot{F}\Vert  &= \sum\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot (-(u \leftrightarrow v)) = 0\rbrace \\
    &= \sum\lbrace b \in B : b \cdot (u \symdiff v) = 0\rbrace \\
    &= \Vert u \symdiff v \in \dot{I}\Vert
    \end{align*}$$
    as desired.
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>
  </div>

    We first note that by Theorem 16.4, $P * \dot{Q}$ satisfies the c.c.c., so all cardinals are preserved in any generic extension. Thus, when mentioning a cardinal $\kappa$ we need not distinguish between $\kappa^V$ and $\kappa^{V[G]}$.<br/>
    <br/>
    Let $\lbrace (p_\alpha,\dot{q}_\alpha) : \alpha < \omega_1\rbrace$ be an uncountable set of conditions of $P * \dot{Q}$. Let $\dot{Z} \in V^P$ be the canonical name for the set $\lbrace \alpha : p_\alpha \in G\rbrace$, where $G$ is a generic filter on $P$ (i.e. $\Vert \alpha \in \dot{Z}\Vert  = p_\alpha$ for all $\alpha < \omega_1$). We also let $\dot{F} \in V^P$ be the name of a function such that $\Vert (\check{\alpha},\dot{q}_\alpha) \in \dot{F}\Vert  = p_\alpha$ for all $\alpha < \omega_1$.<br/>
    <br/>
    Since $\Vert \check{\alpha} \in \dot{Z}\Vert  = p_\alpha > 0$ for all $\alpha$, we have that $\Vert \dot{Z} \subseteq \check{\beta}\Vert  \neq 1$ for all $\beta < \omega_1$. Thus, there exists a generic filter $G$ such that $V[G] \models \vert Z_G\vert  = \aleph_1$. We fix this $G$. Now $V[G] \models \dot{Q}$ has property (K), so there exists $W \subseteq Z$ such that in $V[G]$, $W$ is uncountable and all elements in $\lbrace F_G(\alpha) : \alpha \in W\rbrace$ are pairwise compatible. Fix some $p \in G$ such that $p \forces$ $\dot{W} \subseteq \dot{Z}$ and all elements in $\lbrace \dot{F}(\alpha) : \alpha \in \dot{W}\rbrace$ are pairwise compatible.<br/>
    <br/>
    From here we work in the ground $V$. Let:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    W' := \lbrace \alpha < \omega_1 : (\exists p' \in P)(p' \leq p \wedge p' \leq p_\alpha \wedge p' \forces \alpha \in \dot{W})\rbrace
    \end{align*}$$
    We see that $W'$ is uncountable â€” in $V[G]$, if $\alpha \in W_G$ then $p' \forces \check{\alpha} \in \dot{W}$ for some $p' \in G$, and since $G$ is a filter we may choose $p'$ with $p' \leq p$ and $p' \leq p_\alpha$. Since all cardinalities are preserved, $W'$ is uncountable in $V$. For each $\alpha \in W'$ fix $p_\alpha'$ with $p_\alpha' \leq p$, $p_\alpha' \leq p_\alpha$ and $p_\alpha' \forces \alpha \in \dot{W}$. Since $P$ has property (K), let $W'' \subseteq W'$ be uncountable such that all elements in $W''$ are pairwise compatible.<br/>
    <br/>
    We show that $\lbrace (p_\alpha,\dot{q}_\alpha) : \alpha \in W''\rbrace$ are pairwise compatible. Fix $\alpha, \beta \in W''$. Since $p_\alpha'$ and $p_\beta'$ are compatible, let $p' \leq p_\alpha'$ and $p' \leq p_\beta'$. Then $p' \forces (\check{\alpha} \in \dot{W} \wedge \check{\beta} \in \dot{W})$, and since $p' \leq p$, $p' \forces$ all elements in $\lbrace \dot{F}(\alpha) : \alpha \in \dot{W}\rbrace$ are pairwise compatible. Since $V^P$ is full, there exists $\dot{q} \in \dot{Q}$ such that $p' \forces (\dot{q} \leq \dot{q}_\alpha \wedge \dot{q} \leq \dot{q}_\beta)$. Then $(p',\dot{q}) \leq (p_\alpha,\dot{q}_\alpha)$ and $(p',\dot{q}) \leq (p_\beta,\dot{q}_\beta)$.
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>

    \begin{align*}
    \forces_\beta (\exists q \leq p(\beta)) \, U_q \cap U(\beta) = \emptyset
    \end{align*}$$

    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \forces_\beta U_{\dot{q}} \cap U(\beta) = \emptyset
    \end{align*}$$

Let $U_{q'} \in B$ be the regular cut of $P_\alpha$ generated by $q'$. It's easy to see that $\forces_\beta U_{q'}(\beta) = U_{\dot{q}}$, and $\forces_\gamma U_{q'}(\gamma) = \dot{Q}_\gamma$ for all $\gamma \neq \beta$. Therefore $U_{q'} \cap U = \emptyset$, so $U$ is regular.

<div class="semester-section">
  <a name="ex16.13"></a>
  <h3 class="semester-title">Exercise 16.13</h3>
  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    $\implies$ is of course immediate. The proof of the converse follows closely to that of Lemma 16.12. Suppose $\MA_\kappa$ holds for partial orders of cardinality $\leq\kappa$. Let $P$ be a c.c.c. partially ordered set, and let $\mathcal{D}$ be a family of at most $\kappa$ dense subsets of $P$. Let $W_D$ be the maximal incompatible subset of $D$, which is countable as $P$ satisfies the c.c.c.
    <div class="lemma-box" style="margin-top: 12px;">
      <span class="status-badge lemma">Claim</span>
      <br/>
      There exists some $Q \subseteq P$ such that:
      <ol style="margin: 8px 0; padding-left: 1.5em;">
        <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">$\vert Q\vert  \leq \kappa$.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">For all $D \in \mathcal{D}$, $W_D \subseteq Q$ and is a maximal antichain in $Q$.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">For all $p, q \in Q$ compatible, there exists an $r \in Q$ such that $r \leq p$ and $r \leq q$.</li>
      </ol>
      <hr/>
      <span class="status-badge lemma">Proof</span>
      <br/>
      Let $W := \bigcup_{D \in \mathcal{D}} W_D$. Since $\vert \mathcal{D}\vert  \leq \kappa$ and $\vert W_D\vert  \leq \aleph_0$ for all $D \in \mathcal{D}$, we have $\vert W\vert  \leq \kappa$. Define $Q_n$ inductively: Let $Q_0 := W \cup \lbrace 1\rbrace$. Assume $Q_n$ is constructed with $\vert Q_n\vert   \leq \kappa$. Let $f_n$ be a choice function on $Q_n \times Q_n$ such that for all $p, q$, if $p$ and $q$ are compatible then $f_n(p,q) \leq p$ and $f_n(p,q) \leq q$ (otherwise $f_n(p,q) = 1$). Let $Q_{n+1} := Q_n \cup \ran(f_n)$, so $\vert Q_{n+1}\vert  \leq \kappa$.<br/>
      <br/>
      Let $Q := \bigcup_{n\in\omega} Q_n$. Since $\kappa$ is regular, $\vert Q\vert  \leq \kappa$. Clearly $Q$ satisfies (ii). For (iii), if $p, q \in Q$ are compatible then $p, q \in Q_n$ for some $n$, so $r := f_n(p,q) \in Q_{n+1}$.
      <p align="right">$\blacksquare$</p>
    </div>
    Since $E_D := \lbrace q \in Q : q \leq w \text{ for some } w \in W_D\rbrace$ is dense for all $D \in \mathcal{D}$, each $W_D$ is a maximal antichain in $Q$. Then $\vert Q\vert  \leq \kappa$ and $Q$ satisfies c.c.c., so by $\MA_\kappa$ there exists a filter $G$ intersecting all of the $E_D$'s. Then $w \in G$ for some $w \in W_D \subseteq D$ by upward closure, so the filter in $P$ generated by $G$ is $\mathcal{D}$-generic.
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>
