---

<div class="reviews-container">

  <p class="reviews-intro">
    I only started devoting myself to set theory in the second semester of my junior year, but thanks to various resources, I was able to pick up the basics rather quickly. Here are some of these resources. Note that these were my views on the respective books as an undergraduate, but I have briefly included my revised opinions as a fourth-year PhD student.
  </p>

  <div class="reviews-list">

    <div class="book-card">
      <h3 class="book-header">Kenneth Kunen, <em>Set Theory</em> (2011)</h3>
      <div class="book-body">
        <div class="book-cover-wrapper">
          <img src="/pics/Cover_Kunen2011.jpg" alt="Set Theory by Kenneth Kunen (2011) Cover" class="book-cover-img">
        </div>
        <div class="book-content">
          <p>This is the book I used when I first started learning set theory.</p>
          <p>This book excels at conveying highly abstract set-theoretic concepts in an intuitive manner. Set theory and topology share many similarities, and the book occasionally reminds the reader of these connections. If you are a fan of analysis and have a background in topology (like I did back in my junior year), I believe this book is for you.</p>
          <p>However, this book uses many nonstandard notations (e.g. $R(\alpha)$ instead of $V_\alpha$, $L(\alpha)$ instead of $L_\alpha$, $L(\alpha)[A]$ instead of $L_\alpha(A)$ etc.), so readers must be careful about the standard notations when reading it.</p>
          <p>The exercises tend to be either too easy or too difficult, and not many hints are provided.</p>
          <p>I strongly recommend this book to anyone interested in learning set theory.</p>
          
          <div class="phd-opinion-box">
            <strong>Opinion as a PhD student.</strong> Nowadays, I rarely refer to this book, but I still believe that this is an excellent book to begin one’s journey in set theory.
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>
    </div>

    <div class="book-card">
      <h3 class="book-header">Thomas Jech, <em>Set Theory</em> (2003)</h3>
      <div class="book-body">
        <div class="book-cover-wrapper">
          <img src="/pics/Cover_Jech.jpg" alt="Set Theory by Thomas Jech Cover" class="book-cover-img">
        </div>
        <div class="book-content">
          <p>This is the book that is always by my side these days.</p>
          <p>This book is a set theory bible. Much of the theory in this book remains relevant nowadays.</p>
          <p>Most of the book is very detailed and well-written, but the first six chapters are too brief for beginners to study. I recommend that beginners start with Kunen (above) and then proceed with this book from Chapter 7 onwards. However, Jech’s approach to forcing uses Boolean algebras. While the theory of forcing with Boolean algebras is extremely elegant, it is rarely used in practice; therefore, I recommend that readers refer to Kunen’s book (2011) for a more comprehensive treatment of forcing. Regardless, it is worth learning forcing with Boolean algebras at least once.</p>
          <p>This book contains numerous exercises with helpful hints. They are also reasonably difficult, and I recommend that readers attempt as many of them as possible.</p>
          
          <div class="phd-opinion-box">
            <strong>Opinion as a PhD student.</strong> I still refer to this book regularly.
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>
    </div>

    <div class="book-card">
      <h3 class="book-header">Akihiro Kanamori, <em>The Higher Infinite</em> (2003)</h3>
      <div class="book-body">
        <div class="book-cover-wrapper">
          <img src="/pics/Cover_Kanamori.jpg" alt="The Higher Infinite by Akihiro Kanamori Cover" class="book-cover-img">
        </div>
        <div class="book-content">
          <p>This book is similar to Jech’s book, but it focuses on large cardinals.</p>
          <p>I don’t read this book very often, but I feel that it complements Jech’s book very well (i.e., in parts where Jech’s book is not clear, reading Kanamori’s book usually clarifies my doubts).</p>
          <p>I attempted only a few exercises from this book.</p>
          
          <div class="phd-opinion-box">
            <strong>Opinion as a PhD student.</strong> I rarely refer to this book these days, as I rarely touch on large cardinals in my research. However, I like its treatment of determinacy Chapter 6.
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>
    </div>

    <div class="book-card">
      <h3 class="book-header">Kenneth Kunen, <em>Set Theory: An Introduction to Independence Proofs</em> (1980)</h3>
      <div class="book-body">
        <div class="book-cover-wrapper">
          <img src="/pics/Cover_Kunen1980.jpg" alt="Set Theory (1980) by Kenneth Kunen Cover" class="book-cover-img">
        </div>
        <div class="book-content">
          <p>This is the predecessor of Kunen’s 2011 Set Theory.</p>
          <p>I don’t have much to say about this book, but compared to the 2011 version, I feel it introduces concepts in a somewhat unmotivated manner. I found it very difficult to get through Chapter 3.</p>
          <p>Contrary to the opinions of many logicians I’ve spoken to, I believe that the 2011 version is superior to this one.</p>
          
          <div class="phd-opinion-box">
            <strong>Opinion as a PhD student.</strong> I don't think I've opened this book since my undergraduate days.
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>
    </div>

    <div class="book-card">
      <h3 class="book-header">Lorenz J. Halbeisen, <em>Combinatorial Set Theory: With a Gentle Introduction to Forcing</em> (2018)</h3>
      <div class="book-body">
        <div class="book-cover-wrapper">
          <img src="/pics/Cover_Halbeisen.jpg" alt="Combinatorial Set Theory by Lorenz J. Halbeisen Cover" class="book-cover-img">
        </div>
        <div class="book-content">
          <p>This is a very recent book on combinatorial set theory. It also serves as a bible, but (unsurprisingly) focuses on infinitary combinatorics. This book is especially helpful if you’re looking for various properties (e.g. proper, adds a dominating real) of common forcings. The proofs are detailed and helpful, and the book’s recency also makes it one of the more updated bibles.</p>
          <p>I strongly do not recommend starting on set theory, or even self-studying in general, with this book (yes, I tried).</p>
          
          <div class="phd-opinion-box">
            <strong>Opinion as a PhD student.</strong> This is the book that I refer to the most these days. It is an extremely helpful guide that details various important basic properties of different types of forcings. While I still do not think this book is suitable for beginners, I strongly recommend PhD students in set theory to use this book as a regular reference.
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>
    </div>

  </div>
