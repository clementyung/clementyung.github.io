  <h3 class="semester-title">Exercise 14.1</h3>
  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    We shall show that the $D$ in the hint is dense (for fixed $p, q \in P$). This gives the conclusion, as if $G$ is generic then $G \cap D \neq \emptyset$, so we have $r \in G \cap D$. Since all elements in $G$ are compatible, neither $r$ incompatible with $p$ nor $r$ incompatible with $q$ is possible. It follows that $r \leq p$ and $r \leq q$, as desired.<br/>
    <br/>
    Let $s \in P$, and we consider two cases. If $s$ is incompatible with $p$ or $q$, then by definition of $D$, $s \in D$. Otherwise, $s$ is compatible with both $p$ and $q$, so there exists $t \in P$ such that $t \leq s, p, q$. Then, in particular, $t \leq p, q$ so $t \in D$, and $t \leq s$.
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>
  </div>

  <a name="ex14.2"></a>
  <h3 class="semester-title">Exercise 14.2</h3>
  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    <u>$\implies$:</u> Fix $p \in G$. Let $D \in M$ be dense below $p$. Define:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    D' := \lbrace q \in P : q \perp p \vee q \in D\rbrace
    \end{align*}$$
    We see that $D'$ is dense in $P$. Indeed, for any $r \in P$, if $r \perp p$ then $r \in D'$. Otherwise, there exists $s \in P$ such that $s \leq p, r$. Since $D$ is dense below $p$, there exists $t \in D'$ such that $t \leq s \leq r$, as desired.<br/>
    <br/>
    Thus, we have that $G \cap D' \neq \emptyset$. Let $q \in G \cap D'$. But since $p \in G$, we must have $q \not\perp p$, so $q \in D$. Hence $q \in G \cap D$.<br/>
    <br/>
    <u>$\impliedby$:</u> Fix $D \in M$ dense in $P$, and let $p \in G$ be arbitrary. Consider:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    D' := \lbrace q \in D : q \leq p\rbrace
    \end{align*}$$
    Clearly $D'$ is dense below $p$, so by hypothesis $\emptyset \neq G \cap D' \subseteq G \cap D$.
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>
  </div>

<div class="semester-section">
  <a name="ex14.9"></a>
  <h3 class="semester-title">Exercise 14.9</h3>
  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    We use the notations/definitions in the proof of Lemma 14.11. Fix a poset $(P,<)$ and let $(Q,<')$ be a separative quotient of $(P,<)$, along with an onto embedding $h : P \to Q$. It suffices to show that $(Q,<')$ is isomorphic to $({P/\!\sim},\prec)$, where $\sim$ is defined as in Lemma 14.11.<br/>
    <br/>
    For $x \in P$, define $g : {P/\!\sim} \to Q$ by:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    g([x]) = h(x)
    \end{align*}$$
    We show that this is well-defined. Suppose $x \sim y$ for some $x, y \in P$. Then for all $z \in P$, we have $z$ is compatible with $x$ iff $z$ is compatible with $y$. Thus $h(z)$ is compatible with $h(x)$ iff $h(z)$ is compatible with $h(y)$. Since $(Q,<')$ is separative and $h$ is onto, we have that $h(x) = h(y)$. By a reversed reasoning, we may also conclude that $g$ is one-to-one.<br/>
    <br/>
    We now show that $g$ is order-preserving. Suppose $[x] \preceq [y]$, so $(\forall z \leq x)$[$z$ and $y$ are compatible]. This implies that, in $(Q,<')$, for all $z \leq h(x)$ we have that $z$ and $h(y)$ are compatible. By the hint again, this implies that $h(x) \leq h(y)$, as desired.
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>
  </div>
</div>

  <a name="ex14.10"></a>
  <h3 class="semester-title">Exercise 14.10</h3>
  <div class="lemma-box">
    <a name="lem14.10.A"></a>
    <span class="status-badge lemma">Lemma 14.10.A</span>
    <br/>
    Let $X \subseteq B^+$ and define:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    D := \lbrace u \in B^+ : (\forall x \in X) \, u \leq x\rbrace \cup \lbrace u \in B^+ : (\exists x \in X) \, u \leq -x\rbrace
    \end{align*}$$
    Then $D$ is an open dense subset of $B^+$.
    <hr/>
    <span class="status-badge lemma">Proof</span>
    <br/>
    Openness of $D$ is clear. Let $u \in B^+$, and we consider two cases. If $u \leq x$ for all $x \in X$, then immediately we have $u \in D$. Otherwise, $u \not\leq x$ implies that $u \cdot (-x) \neq 0$ for some $x \in X$. Then $u \cdot (-x) \leq -x$, and $u \cdot (-x) \in D$.
    <p align="right">$\blacksquare$</p>
  </div>
  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    Note that $0 \notin G$ so $G \subseteq B^+$. It's easy to see that $G$ is a filter on $B$ iff $G$ is a filter on $B^+$.<br/>
    <br/>
    <u>$\implies$:</u> Note that $G \subseteq B^+$ remains an ultrafilter. Let $D \subseteq B^+$ be open dense. Suppose $G \cap D = \emptyset$. Then $X := \lbrace -u : u \in D\rbrace \subseteq G$, and since $G$ is generic in $B$, $\prod X \in G$. If $\prod X = 0$, then we have a contradiction immediately. Otherwise, $\prod X \in B^+$, so by denseness of $D$ we have that $v \leq \prod X$ for some $v \in D$. Then $v \leq -v$ and $v \neq 0$, a contradiction as well.<br/>
    <br/>
    <u>$\impliedby$:</u> We first show that $G$ is an ultrafilter. Let $u \in B$ and let:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    D := \lbrace v \in B^+ : v \leq u \vee v \cdot u = 0\rbrace
    \end{align*}$$
    By <a href="#lem14.10.A">Lemma 14.10.A</a>, $D$ is open dense. Since $G$ is generic in $B^+$, we have $v \in G \cap D$ for some $v \in B^+$. If $v \leq u$ then $u \in G$ by upward closure. Otherwise, $v \cdot u = 0$ so $v \leq -u$, hence $-u \in G$.<br/>
    <br/>
    Now let $X \subseteq G$. Consider:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    D := \lbrace u \in B^+ : (\forall x \in X) \, u \leq x\rbrace \cup \lbrace u \in B^+ : (\exists x \in X) \, u \leq -x\rbrace
    \end{align*}$$
    Again by <a href="#lem14.10.A">Lemma 14.10.A</a>, $D$ is open dense in $B^+$. Since $G$ is generic in $B^+$, we have that $v \in G \cap D$ for some $v \in B^+$. By compatibility of elements in $G$, we can't have $v \leq -x$ for any $x \in X \subseteq G$. Thus, we must have $v \leq x$ for all $x \in X$. By upward closure (and completeness of $B$), $\prod X \in G$.
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>
  </div>
</div>

  <h3 class="semester-title">Exercise 14.12</h3>
  <a name="ex14.12(i)"></a>
  <p class="sub-exercise-title">Exercise 14.12(i)</p>
  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Statement</span>
    <br/>
    $\Vert(\exists y \in x) \, \varphi(y)\Vert = \sum_{y \in \dom{x}} (x(y) \cdot \Vert\varphi(y)\Vert)$.
    <hr/>
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert(\exists y \in x) \, \varphi(y)\Vert &= \Vert\exists y \, (y \in x \wedge \varphi(y))\Vert \\
    &= \sum_{y \in V^B} \Vert y \in x \wedge \varphi(y)\Vert \\
    &= \sum_{y \in V^B} \Vert y \in x\Vert \cdot \Vert\varphi(y)\Vert \\
    &= \sum_{y \in V^B} \bb{\sum_{t \in \dom{x}} \Vert y = t\Vert \cdot x(t)} \cdot \Vert\varphi(y)\Vert \\
    &= \sum_{t \in \dom{x}} x(t) \cdot \bb{\sum_{y \in V^B} \Vert y = t\Vert \cdot \Vert\varphi(y)\Vert} \\
    &= \sum_{t \in \dom{x}} x(t) \cdot \bb{\sum_{y \in V^B} \Vert y = t \wedge \varphi(y)\Vert} \\
    &= \sum_{t \in \dom{x}} x(t) \cdot \bb{\sum_{y \in V^B} \Vert y = t \wedge \varphi(t)\Vert} \\
    &= \sum_{t \in \dom{x}} x(t) \cdot \bb{\sum_{y \in V^B} \Vert y = t\Vert \cdot \Vert\varphi(t)\Vert} \\
    &= \sum_{t \in \dom{x}} x(t) \cdot \Vert\varphi(t)\Vert \cdot \bb{\sum_{y \in V^B} \Vert y = t\Vert} \\
    &= \sum_{t \in \dom{x}} x(t) \cdot \Vert\varphi(t)\Vert \cdot \Vert\exists y \, (y = t)\Vert \\
    &= \sum_{t \in \dom{x}} x(t) \cdot \Vert\varphi(t)\Vert
    \end{align*}$$
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>
  </div>
  <a name="ex14.12(ii)"></a>
  <p class="sub-exercise-title">Exercise 14.12(ii)</p>
  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    We use $\to$ in place of $\Rightarrow$, and apply <a href="https://clementyung.github.io/jech-solutions/chapter-7#ex7.27">Exercise 7.27</a> and <a href="#ex14.12(i)">Exercise 14.12(i)</a>.
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert (\forall y \in x) \, \varphi(y)\Vert  &= -\Vert (\exists y \in x) \, \neg\varphi(y)\Vert  \\
    &= -\bb{\sum_{y \in \dom{x}} x(y) \cdot \Vert \neg\varphi(y)\Vert } \\
    &= \prod_{y \in \dom(x)} -(x(y) \cdot \Vert \neg\varphi(y)\Vert ) \\
    &= \prod_{y \in \dom(x)} (-x(y) + (-\Vert \neg\varphi(y)\Vert )) \\
    &= \prod_{y \in \dom(x)} (-x(y) + \Vert \varphi(y)\Vert ) \\
    &= \prod_{y \in \dom(x)} (x(y) \to \Vert \varphi(y)\Vert )
    \end{align*}$$
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>
  </div>

  <h3 class="semester-title">Exercise 14.13</h3>
  <a name="ex14.13(i)"></a>
  <p class="sub-exercise-title">Exercise 14.13(i)</p>
  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Statement</span>
    <br/>
    If $x = y$ then $\Vert\check{x} = \check{y}\Vert = 1$ and if $x \neq y$ then $\Vert\check{x} = \check{y}\Vert = 0$.
    <hr/>
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    It is likely that Jech wanted to use these exercises as the base case for the proof of Lemma 14.21, so we shall not use that result here. For convenience, we combine the solution of this exercise with <a href="#ex14.13(ii)">Exercise 14.13(ii)</a>.<br/>
    <br/>
    We induct on $\rho(x)$, the rank of $x$ in $V^B$. If $x = y$, then clearly $\check{x} = \check{y}$ so $\Vert\check{x} = \check{y}\Vert = 1$ by Lemma 14.15 (induction hypothesis not required).<br/>
    <br/>
    If $x \in y$, we have $\Vert\check{x} \in \check{y}\Vert = \sum_{t \in \check{y}} (\Vert\check{x} = t\Vert \cdot \check{y}(t))$. By definition, $\check{x} \in \dom(\check{y})$ with $\check{y}(\check{x}) = 1$. Thus:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert\check{x} \in \check{y}\Vert \geq \Vert\check{x} = \check{x}\Vert \cdot \check{y}(\check{x}) = 1 \cdot 1 = 1
    \end{align*}$$
    Induction hypothesis is not required here either.<br/>
    <br/>
    If $x \neq y$, WLOG suppose $x - y$ is non-empty. Let $z \in x - y$. Note that $\rho(z) < \rho(x)$, so by induction hypothesis $\Vert\check{z} \in \check{y}\Vert = 0$. Then:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert\check{x} \subseteq \check{y}\Vert &= \prod_{t \in \dom{\check{x}}} (\check{x}(t) \to \Vert t \in \check{y}\Vert) \\
    &\leq \check{x}(\check{z}) \to \Vert\check{z} \in \check{y}\Vert \\
    &= (-1) + 0 \\
    &= 0
    \end{align*}$$
    so $\Vert\check{x} = \check{y}\Vert \leq \Vert\check{x} \subseteq \check{y}\Vert = 0$.<br/>
    <br/>
    If $x \notin y$, then by the $x \neq y$ case above we have:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert\check{x} \in \check{y}\Vert &= \sum_{t \in \dom{\check{y}}} \Vert t = \check{x}\Vert \cdot \check{y}(t) \\
    &= \sum_{z \in y} \Vert\check{z} = \check{x}\Vert \cdot \check{y}(\check{z}) \\
    &= \sum_{z \in y} 0 \cdot \check{y}(\check{z}) \\
    &= 0
    \end{align*}$$
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>
  </div>

<div class="semester-section">
  <a name="ex14.14"></a>
  <h3 class="semester-title">Exercise 14.14</h3>
  <a name="ex14.14(i)"></a>
  <p class="sub-exercise-title">Exercise 14.14(i)</p>
  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    Recall that the statement "$G$ is an ultrafilter on $B$" is equivalent to the conjunction of the following:
    <ol style="margin: 8px 0; padding-left: 1.5em;">
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">$1 \in G$, $0 \notin G$.</li>
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">$(\forall u \in G)(\forall v \in B)(u \leq v \to v \in G)$.</li>
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">$(\forall u \in G)(\forall v \in G) \, u \cdot v \in G$.</li>
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">$(\forall u \in B)(u \in G \vee -u \in G)$.</li>
    </ol>
    Thus, it suffices to show that all the statements above have Boolean values $1$. WLOG we may assume that $B$ is a complete Boolean algebra of sets, so by Lemma 14.21 we have that for $u, v \in B$, $u \leq v$ iff $\Vert \check{u} \leq \check{v}\Vert  = 1$. This implies that for any $u \in B$:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert \check{u} \in \dot{G}\Vert  = \sum_{v \in B} \Vert \check{u} = \check{v}\Vert  \cdot \dot{G}(\check{v}) = \dot{G}(\check{u}) = u
    \end{align*}$$
    <ol style="margin: 8px 0; padding-left: 1.5em;">
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">We have $\Vert \check{1} \in \dot{G}\Vert  = \dot{G}(\check{1}) = 1$ and $\Vert \check{0} \in \dot{G}\Vert  = \dot{G}(\check{0}) = 0$.</li>
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">Using <a href="#ex14.12(ii)">Exercise 14.12(ii)</a>:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        &\eqbreak \Vert (\forall u \in \dot{G})(\forall v \in \check{B})(u \leq v \to v \in \dot{G})\Vert  \\
        &= \prod_{u \in \dom{\dot{G}}} \bb{\dot{G}(u) \to \Vert (\forall v \in \check{B})(u \leq v \to v \in \dot{G})\Vert } \\
        &= \prod_{u \in B} \bb{(-u) + \Vert (\forall v \in \check{B})(\check{u} \leq v \to v \in \dot{G})\Vert }
        \end{align*}$$
        Fix a $u \in B$. Then:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \Vert (\forall v \in \check{B})(\check{u} \leq v \to v \in \dot{G})\Vert  &= \prod_{v \in \dom{\check{B}}} (\check{B}(v) \to \Vert \check{u} \leq v \to v \in \dot{G}\Vert ) \\
        &= \prod_{v \in B} ((-1) + \Vert \check{u} \leq \check{v} \to \check{v} \in \dot{G}\Vert ) \\
        &= \prod_{v \in B} \Vert \check{u} \leq \check{v} \to \check{v} \in \dot{G}\Vert  \\
        &= \prod_{v \in B} ((-\Vert \check{u} \leq \check{v}\Vert ) + \Vert \check{v} \in \dot{G}\Vert )
        \end{align*}$$
        Now if $v \not\geq u$, then $-\Vert \check{u} \leq \check{v}\Vert  = 1$, so it suffices to consider products over $v \geq u$. Thus:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \prod_{v \in B} ((-\Vert \check{u} \leq \check{v}\Vert ) + \Vert \check{v} \in \dot{G}\Vert ) &= \prod_{\substack{v \in B \\ u \leq v}} \dot{G}(\check{v}) \\
        &= \prod_{\substack{v \in B \\ u \leq v}} v \\
        &= u
        \end{align*}$$
        Therefore:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \prod_{u \in B} \bb{(-u) + \Vert (\forall v \in \check{B})(\check{u} \leq v \to v \in \dot{G})\Vert } = \prod_{u \in B} ((-u) + u) = 1
        \end{align*}$$
      </li>
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">We have:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \Vert (\forall u \in \dot{G})(\forall v \in \dot{G}) \, u \cdot v \in \dot{G}\Vert  = \prod_{u \in B} \bb{(-u) + \Vert (\forall v \in \dot{G}) \, \check{u} \cdot v \in \dot{G}\Vert }
        \end{align*}$$
        So we fix $u \in B$. Then:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \Vert (\forall v \in \dot{G}) \, \check{u} \cdot v \in \dot{G}\Vert  = \prod_{v \in B} \bb{(-v) + \Vert \check{u} \cdot \check{v} \in \dot{G}\Vert }
        \end{align*}$$
        Since $B$ is an algebra of sets, $\check{u} \cdot \check{v} = (u \cdot v)\check{\phantom{x}}$. Thus:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \prod_{v \in B} \bb{(-v) + \Vert \check{u} \cdot \check{v} \in \dot{G}\Vert } &= \prod_{v \in B} \bb{(-v) + u \cdot v} \\
        &\geq \prod_{v \in B} \bb{u \cdot (-v) + u \cdot v} \\
        &= \prod_{v \in B} u \\
        &= u
        \end{align*}$$
        Therefore:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \prod_{u \in B} \bb{(-u) + \Vert (\forall v \in \dot{G}) \, \check{u} \cdot v \in \dot{G}\Vert } \geq \prod_{u \in B} ((-u) + u) = 1
        \end{align*}$$
      </li>
      <li style="margin-bottom: 6px;">We have:
        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \Vert (\forall u \in \check{B})(\check{u} \in \dot{G} \vee -\check{u} \in \dot{G})\Vert  &= \prod_{u \in B} ((-1) + \Vert \check{u} \in \dot{G} \vee -\check{u} \in \dot{G}\Vert ) \\
        &= \prod_{u \in B} (\Vert \check{u} \in \dot{G}\Vert  + \Vert {-\check{u}} \in \dot{G}\Vert ) \\
        &= \prod_{u \in B} (u + (-u)) \\
        &= \prod_{u \in B} 1 \\
        &= 1
        \end{align*}$$
      </li>
    </ol>
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>
  </div>
  <a name="ex14.14(ii)"></a>
  <p class="sub-exercise-title">Exercise 14.14(ii)</p>
  <div class="solution-box">
    <span class="status-badge success">Solution</span>
    <br/>
    We first note that since $B$ is complete, and $\Vert \dot{G} \text{ is an ultrafilter on } B\Vert  = 1$ by <a href="#ex14.14(i)">Exercise 14.14(i)</a>, we have that:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert\prod X \in \dot{G}\Vert  &= \Vert (\exists u \in \dot{G})(\forall x \in \check{X}) \, u \leq x\Vert  \\
    &= \sum_{u \in B}(\dot{G}(\check{u}) \cdot \Vert (\forall x \in \check{X}) \, \check{u} \leq x\Vert ) \\
    &= \sum_{u \in B}(u \cdot \Vert (\forall x \in \check{X}) \, \check{u} \leq x\Vert )
    \end{align*}$$
    Now for each $u \in B$, we have $\Vert (\forall x \in \check{X}) \, \check{u} \leq x\Vert  = \prod_{x \in X} \Vert \check{u} \leq \check{x}\Vert $. Observe that the RHS is $1$ iff $u \leq \prod X$, and $0$ otherwise. Thus:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \sum_{u \in B}(u \cdot \Vert (\forall x \in \check{X}) \, \check{u} \leq x\Vert ) = \sum_{u \leq \prod X} u = \prod X
    \end{align*}$$
    On the other hand, we have:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert \check{X} \subseteq \dot{G}\Vert  &= \Vert (\forall x \in \check{X}) \, x \in \dot{G} \Vert  \\
    &= \prod_{x \in X} \Vert \check{x} \in \dot{G}\Vert  \\
    &= \prod_{x \in X} x \\
    &= \prod X
    \end{align*}$$
    Thus:
    $$
    \begin{align*}
    \Vert \text{if $\check{X} \subseteq \dot{G}$ then $\prod X \in \dot{G}$}\Vert  &= -\Vert \check{X} \subseteq \dot{G}\Vert  + \Vert\prod X \in \dot{G}\Vert  \\
    &= -\prod X + \prod X \\
    &= 1
    \end{align*}$$
    <p align="right">$\square$</p>
